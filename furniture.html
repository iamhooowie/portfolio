ReDesign: Furniture store
Role: Self-Led Project # User Experience # User Interface # Website

In the spring of 2025, I completed an interaction design course focused on brand positioning, target audience analysis, and strategic approaches to digital product design. Throughout the semester, our main project was to redesign an existing website using the methods and techniques learned in class. The goal was to create an innovative solution that effectively communicates the brand’s value, enhances user experience, and supports the client’s business objectives.

Discovery

Jerusalem Furniture is a furniture store offering a wide selection of pieces ranging from classic to contemporary styles. It provides furniture for every living space, including bedroom sets, dining tables and chairs, and decorative items. With a focus on quality and affordability, Jerusalem Furniture provides residents of Boston and surrounding areas with a reliable and stylish option for furnishing their homes.

Challenge

The current website suffers from disorganized design and poor content layout strategies, which confuse visitors, reduce trust in the brand, and risk losing potential customers. Key issues include:

  • Unstrategic Navigation: As an e-commerce website, the navigation structure plays a crucial role. However, content with low relevance to online shopping is placed in prominent positions, instead of being moved to secondary areas where it would be more appropriate.
  • Unengaging Banners and Promotional Content: The hero section lacks banners related to featured products or current promotions. Although the site emphasizes financing options for furniture purchases, the content appears rigid and overly direct—failing to capture users’ attention or drive engagement, resulting in missed conversion opportunities.
  • Product Page: There is no proper product browsing page—only catalogs are available for preview. This results in a poor user experience, as visitors cannot accurately view product details or compare furniture items with others.
  • Lack of Clear Explanation for Key Selling Point: The original website highlights financing support as a main feature, but fails to provide detailed information on how it works. On the relevant page, there are no clear instructions or descriptions, leaving potential customers unsure about how to proceed with a purchase. This may result in lost sales, even among users who are already interested in buying.

Additional problems can be found throughout the website in areas such as visual layout, content structure, and overall usability—for example, the “Contact Us” page, shopping cart, and member-related features all present various user experience challenges. Therefore, addressing the above pain points is essential not only to improve the overall user experience, but also to better support the site’s business goals.

Persona and Journey Map

Linda Halimi, a 52-year-old “savvy budgeter,” represents the brand’s most important customer segment. She is a married part-time administrative assistant living in the suburbs of Boston. With moderate tech literacy, she primarily gathers shopping information through Facebook and Google. Linda cares deeply about her family’s quality of life and serves as the main decision-maker for household budgeting and purchases. She believes that furniture doesn’t have to be expensive — as long as it’s durable, stylish, and affordable, it’s a good choice.

The accompanying customer journey map uses Linda’s real-life experience of purchasing a dining table and chair set to illustrate the full process from initial need to final purchase decision. The research identified several key pain points: confusing website navigation, unclear product information, insufficient explanation of installment payment options, and a lack of effective product comparison features. Linda’s core needs include clear product presentation, transparent installment payment details, a simplified shopping process, and a sense of security when making a purchase.

Target Audience

Primary (60%)

This group includes budget-conscious adults aged 45–65, many with stable incomes and years of experience. They’re practical shoppers who value good deals over trendy designs. Most discover furniture through Facebook ads, community groups, and store flyers.

Secondary (30%)

New parents and newlyweds aged 25–35 are redesigning their homes due to life transitions. While managing tight budgets, they prioritize functional and affordable furniture. They’re influenced by bundles, installment options, and trusted after-sales service, often researching online or through parenting communities.

Tertiary (10%)

Young adults aged 22–30 are setting up their first homes with limited budgets but strong design sensibilities. They seek aesthetic, affordable furniture and often look beyond mainstream brands like IKEA. Influenced by social media and drawn to installment plans, they spend time researching the best value-for-money options online.

Design Criteria

I chose Garamond as the heading typeface because it conveys a sense of elegance and timelessness. Its classic serif style adds a touch of sophistication and trustworthiness to the brand, which aligns well with the image of a quality furniture company. The refined letterforms help elevate the visual tone and create a strong first impression, especially when highlighting the brand story or key messages.

Proxima Nova is used for body text due to its clean, modern, and highly legible design. It strikes a balance between geometric precision and a friendly, approachable feel, making it ideal for displaying product information and content-heavy sections. This font reflects the brand’s positioning—affordable yet stylish—and ensures that users can comfortably read and engage with the content across various devices.

Wireframes

ReDesign

A clean and simple homepage design helps visitors quickly find the products they’re looking for. Appropriate use of white space makes browsing comfortable and pressure-free. Different sections feature CTAs that encourage visitors to click and learn more, increasing the chances of turning visitors into customers.

  1. Nav Bar :
    Main Nav: Organize the products into categories so that visitors can quickly find what they’re looking for.
    Secondary Nav: Use smaller and lighter text for the secondary navigation to differentiate it from the primary navigation.
  2. Main Nav: Use storytelling slogans on the banners. Different banners should feature different slogans to attract various target audiences.
  3. Product Categories: Both the banner and the overall layout follow an F-shaped reading pattern. This design aligns with how most users read web pages, helping catch their attention quickly. The product categories are high-level groupings. I chose warm and harmonious tones for the photos here to reflect the brand’s spirit. If there are subcategories, a number is shown for clarity.
  4. Call to Action: “Want to design your room?” This is a CTA encouraging users to download the app. By doing so, they can use AR to visualize how Jerusalem Furniture pieces would look in their space. The image is enlarged to highlight where to access this service. Since part of the target audience is middle-aged users, this design aims to catch their attention and lead them to download the app. Clicking on it will take them directly to the store page.
  5. The highlight of the brand spirit 1: Allowing users to quickly browse different categories of sale items, making the experience more user-friendly. Product info includes buyer ratings. This feature helps grab attention, especially if the product has good reviews.
  6. The highlight of the brand spirit 2: I used relevant images in the finance aid section. The original website emphasizes stress-free furniture shopping, thanks to installment options through their finance aid partner. So I chose images that reinforce this collaboration. As mentioned above, this is an important section. I think it’s necessary to clearly show users the different ways they can apply for finance aid. It helps them realize that buying furniture from Jerusalem Furniture can be a relaxed experience.
  7. Footer: The footer navigation is less important than the header nav, so I placed it at the bottom. I organized it clearly in list form, making it easier for users to find what they need.

Reflection

Redesigning the website for Jerusalem Furniture was not just about modernizing the brand’s visual identity — more importantly, it was about improving the site’s ability to drive online sales. This led me to ask: in an industry where physical experience remains a key differentiator, how can digital UX become a meaningful bridge between brand and customer?

Through research, I discovered that the original site lacked a strategic navigation structure. Key information was buried under complex categories, leaving users feeling lost during product discovery. Additionally, financing options — a highly appealing factor for many customers — were not clearly communicated. This made me reconsider whether the current design truly addressed real user concerns.

One of the biggest challenges came from designing for three distinctly different user groups: budget-conscious middle-aged families, newlyweds or small households, and younger renters who prioritize aesthetics. To better understand their behaviors and pain points, I created detailed personas and customer journey maps. This process helped me turn abstract insights into actionable design strategies and deepened my belief that design is not just about how things look — it’s about making intentional, strategic choices.

This project also taught me to consider financing options as a core part of the e-commerce experience. From content hierarchy and visual cues to interaction flow, every detail had to clearly convey value in a limited space. I learned how to simplify complex information and present it in a way that feels intuitive and trustworthy.

Looking back, the most valuable part of this project was learning how to integrate diverse needs and support the client in aligning design with business goals. If given the chance to iterate further, I’d love to enhance the product comparison feature and improve the search experience — helping users make decisions faster and turning the website into a true digital storefront.

This journey reminded me that great design isn’t just beautiful — it’s understandable, trustworthy, and ultimately, actionable.
